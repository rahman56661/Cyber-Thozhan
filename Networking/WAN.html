Wide Area Network (WAN)

Connecting Networks Across Countries and Continents

WAN = Wide Area Network

A network that covers very large geographical areas like countries, continents, or the entire world.

Simple Explanation

A Wide Area Network connects multiple Local Area Networks and Metropolitan Area Networks across countries, continents, or worldwide.

LAN

Small area coverage (home, office, building)

MAN

City or metropolitan area coverage

WAN

Country, continent or global coverage

WAN is the largest network category, connecting LANs and MANs across vast distances.

WAN Coverage Area

From 100 km to entire world coverage

Can cover countries, continents, or connect networks globally.

WAN Examples (Real World)

The Internet (World's Biggest WAN)

Millions of networks worldwide connected together. YouTube, Google, WhatsApp all operate over this global WAN.

Banking Networks

SBI, HDFC, ICICI banks connecting branches across cities, states, and international locations through WAN.

MNC Companies Private WAN

TCS, Infosys, Amazon, Google connecting offices across India, UK, US, and Singapore using VPN and leased lines.

ATM Network

Bank ATM machines across entire countries connected to central servers through WAN infrastructure.

Telecom Company Networks

Jio, Airtel towers and data centers working across states and countries using WAN technology.

WAN Structure & Technology

WAN primarily uses these technologies:

  • Fiber optic cables (including underwater submarine cables)
  • Satellite links for remote locations
  • Microwave towers for wireless connections
  • Leased lines from telecom providers
  • MPLS networks for efficient data routing

WAN infrastructure is typically created and maintained by ISPs and large telecom companies.

WAN Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Huge geographical coverage
  • Global access to resources
  • Ideal solution for large multinational companies
  • High reliability with multiple redundant routes
  • Supports cloud computing and international communication
  • Centralized data management

Disadvantages

  • Very expensive setup and infrastructure
  • High maintenance costs (satellites, fiber cables)
  • Lower speeds compared to LAN (due to distance)
  • Higher latency (data travels long distances)
  • Complex security requirements
  • Dependency on third-party service providers
